Reflection Assignment

Helping a Friend: Reflection

Take what you learned this week and make it personal. There are no right or wrong answers here — just honest ones.

📝 Instructions

Answer each of the questions below in complete sentences. Aim for at least 4–6 sentences per response — write enough to actually think it through, not just enough to fill a box.

You can submit this assignment by typing your answers directly into Canvas or by uploading them as a Word doc, Google Doc, or PDF.

⚠️ Do not include real names of friends or family members. Use first initials, fake names, or general descriptions like "a friend of mine."

1
Warning Signs

Look back at the four categories of warning signs (physical & mental, mood, school, social). Which category do you think would be easiest to notice in a friend? Which would be hardest? Explain why.

Helping vs. Enabling

In your own words, explain the difference between helping and enabling. Then describe one example of each — they can be from the reading, from a movie or show you've seen, or from your own life. Why is enabling so easy to fall into, even when someone is trying to be a good friend?

Type your response in Canvas or write here on a printed copy...
3
Apply the CARE Framework

Imagine a friend has been showing several warning signs of substance use. Using the CARE framework (Choose, Approach, Really listen, Encourage), write out exactly how you would start the conversation with them. Where would you do it? What's the first sentence you'd actually say out loud?

Type your response in Canvas or write here on a printed copy...
4
Trusted Adults

Identify three trusted adults in your life you could go to if a friend (or you) needed help with substance use. They can be a parent, relative, school counselor, coach, doctor, religious leader, or anyone else. Briefly explain why you chose each one — what makes them trustworthy in this situation?

Type your response in Canvas or write here on a printed copy...
5
The Hard Choice

The reading argued that telling an adult — even when a friend has begged you not to — is sometimes the right thing to do. Do you agree? Why or why not? What would have to be true for you to break that promise? Be honest, even if your honest answer is uncomfortable.

6
Putting It All Together

Looking back across the entire course — Weeks 1 through 6 — what is the single most important thing you've learned about substance use, friendship, or yourself? How might this change something about how you act, talk, or make decisions going forward?
