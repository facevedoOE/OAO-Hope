Substance Abuse Prevention

Helping a Friend: Intervention & Support Skills

When someone you care about is struggling with substance use, knowing what to say — and what not to say — can change everything. This week, you'll learn how to be the kind of friend that actually helps.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

✓Recognize the warning signs that a friend may be struggling with substance use.
✓Understand the difference between helpful support and enabling behavior.
✓Use specific communication strategies to start a hard conversation with a friend.
✓Identify trusted adults and resources where you can get help — for your friend or yourself.
✓Know how to recognize an overdose emergency and respond safely.
1
Section One

Why Friends Are the First Line of Defense

Think about who would notice first if you started acting differently — drifting off, skipping practice, getting quieter at lunch. Probably not your parents. Probably not a teacher. It would be your friends.

Friends see things adults miss. You're with each other in hallways, at games, on group chats late at night, at parties on weekends. You see the small shifts before anyone else. That makes friends incredibly important — and it puts you in a position adults usually aren't in. You have access. You have trust. You have the chance to say something before something gets worse.

By the Numbers
9 in 10
teens say a friend is the first person they'd talk to about a problem
~70%
of teens who got help for substance use say a friend played a role in getting them there

But here's the catch: most teens have never been taught how to help. We're told "say something" — and then left to figure out what to say, when to say it, and what happens after. That's what this lesson is about. Not vague advice. Real strategies.

💭
Reframe

Helping a friend isn't about being a hero, fixing them, or making them stop. It's about staying close enough that they don't have to go through it alone — and connecting them with people who have more tools than you do.

Section Two

Recognizing the Warning Signs

No single sign means a friend is using substances. Teenagers go through moods, growth spurts, breakups, and bad weeks. What you're looking for is a pattern — multiple changes that show up together and stick around.

The signs cluster into four categories. Read them slowly. Picture someone you actually know.

🧠 Physical & Mental Changes

  • Bloodshot or glassy eyes; dilated or pinpoint pupils
  • Sudden weight loss or gain, or stopping basic hygiene
  • Slurred speech, shaky hands, or unusual clumsiness
  • Smelling like smoke, alcohol, or unfamiliar chemicals
  • Falling asleep at strange times or staying up all night

🎭 Mood & Personality Shifts

  • Withdrawing from people they used to be close to
  • Sudden, unexplained anger or irritability
  • New secrecy — about phone, who they're with, where they go
  • Loss of interest in activities they used to love
  • Talking about feeling hopeless, numb, or like nothing matters

📚 School & Performance

  • Grades dropping suddenly with no clear reason
  • Skipping classes, practices, or activities
  • Trouble focusing or remembering simple things
  • Losing a spot on a team, in a club, or in a leadership role

👥 Social & Behavioral Red Flags

  • A whole new friend group — especially if they hide it
  • Lying about small, easily-checked things
  • Money or items going missing from family or friends
  • Risky behaviors: driving impaired, sneaking out, fighting
  • Pill bottles, vape devices, lighters, or unfamiliar packaging in their stuff
⚠️
Important

You don't need to be sure to say something. You don't need proof. If your gut says something is wrong, that's enough. Adults wait for proof and miss things. Friends notice patterns and act early — that's the whole advantage you have.

Section Three

Helping vs. Enabling: The Most Important Distinction

This is where most people who try to help actually make things worse — without realizing it. There's a difference between helping a friend and enabling them.

Helping means doing things that move your friend toward getting better — even when it's uncomfortable. Enabling means doing things that protect them from the consequences of their substance use, which lets the substance use keep going.

✓ Helping
  • Telling a trusted adult, even when your friend asks you not to
  • Saying out loud, "I'm worried about you"
  • Being honest when they ask if you've noticed changes
  • Inviting them to sober activities
  • Staying their friend without using with them
✗ Enabling
  • Lying to their parents to cover for them
  • Doing their homework so no one notices
  • Buying or holding their substances "to keep them safe"
  • Pretending nothing is wrong because you're scared
  • Promising secrecy you can't keep

Enabling almost always comes from a good place. You don't want to get them in trouble. You don't want them to be mad at you. You don't want to lose the friendship. Those are real, valid feelings. But they protect the substance use — not your friend.

🔑
The Core Question

Before you do anything, ask yourself: "Does this make it easier for them to keep using, or harder?" If the answer is "easier," it's enabling — even if it feels like kindness.

4
Section Four

How to Actually Have the Conversation

The hardest part isn't knowing something is wrong. It's opening your mouth. Here's a framework that actually works — used by counselors, peer educators, and intervention specialists. It's called CARE.

C

Choose the right time and place

Not at a party. Not in a hallway between classes. Not over text. Pick a private, calm moment when neither of you is rushed or upset.

Try: a walk, a car ride, sitting on the bleachers after practice.

A

Approach with "I" statements, not "you" accusations

"You" statements feel like attacks and shut conversations down. "I" statements describe what you're seeing without putting them on the defensive.

Don't say: "You're getting high all the time and it's pathetic."
Try: "I've noticed you seem different lately, and I'm worried about you."

R

Really listen — without judging or fixing

If they open up, your job is to listen. Not to lecture. Not to give a speech about brain chemistry. Not to compare them to someone else. Just listen.

Useful phrases: "That sounds really hard." "Thank you for telling me." "I'm not going anywhere."

E

Encourage them to talk to a trusted adult

You're not their therapist. You're their friend. Your goal is to be a bridge to someone with more tools — a parent, a school counselor, a coach, an aunt or uncle, a doctor.

Offer: "I'll go with you if you want." That offer alone changes everything for some people.

😤
"What if they get mad at me?"

They might. People who are using substances often get defensive when someone notices — that's part of how addiction protects itself. A mad friend is way better than a friend in the hospital. You'd rather lose a friendship temporarily than lose a friend forever.

5
Section Five

When You Have to Tell an Adult — Even if You Promised Not To

There's a kind of secret you keep — your friend's crush, an embarrassing story, a bad grade. And there's a kind of secret you don't keep, no matter what you promised: anything that puts their life at risk.

If any of these are happening, tell a trusted adult today — not tomorrow, not after one more conversation, not after they promise to stop. Today.

🚨 Tell an adult immediately if your friend:

  • Has talked about suicide, hurting themselves, or "not being here anymore"
  • Is using substances daily or has lost the ability to stop
  • Has overdosed before, or is using drugs that could be laced with fentanyl
  • Is driving while impaired, or getting in cars with impaired drivers
  • Is mixing substances (alcohol + pills, multiple drugs at once)
  • Has lost or hurt themselves while using
  • Is being abused, exploited, or pressured by an older person who supplies them
💪
This isn't snitching. This is loving them louder than they can yell at you.

Years from now, your friend will not remember being mad at you for telling. They will remember that you cared enough to risk the friendship to keep them alive.

🆘
Critical: Save This

If You Think Someone Is Overdosing

An overdose is a medical emergency. You will not get in trouble for calling 911 to save someone's life. Florida's Good Samaritan law protects people who call for help during a drug or alcohol emergency from being charged for possession.

Signs of Overdose:

  • Slow, shallow, or stopped breathing
  • Blue or gray lips, fingertips, or skin
  • Won't wake up, won't respond when shaken or shouted at
  • Choking, gurgling, or snoring sounds while unconscious
  • Limp body, unusually small pupils (with opioids)

What To Do:

1
Call 911 immediately. Don't wait to see if they "sleep it off." Every second matters.
2
Stay with them. Don't leave them alone, even to "go get help."
3
Roll them on their side (recovery position) so they don't choke if they vomit.
4
If naloxone (Narcan) is available and you suspect opioids, use it. It's a nasal spray, can't hurt them, and can reverse an opioid overdose within minutes.
5
Tell first responders what they took if you know — even if you're scared. It helps them treat your friend faster.

Key Vocabulary

Intervention

A planned, caring conversation with a person struggling with substance use, designed to help them recognize the problem and get help.

Enabling

Actions that protect someone from the natural consequences of their substance use, often unintentionally allowing the use to continue.

Trusted Adult

An older person — parent, counselor, coach, relative — you can talk to about hard things who will listen and help, not punish or panic.

"I" Statement

A communication tool that starts with "I" instead of "you" to share feelings without sounding like an accusation. Example: "I'm worried about you."

Naloxone (Narcan)

A medication, usually a nasal spray, that can quickly reverse an opioid overdose. Available without a prescription in Florida and many other states.

Good Samaritan Law

A law that protects people who call 911 during a drug or alcohol overdose from being charged with possession, so fear of trouble doesn't stop life-saving calls.

Recovery Position

Lying a person on their side with the top knee bent forward to keep their airway open and prevent choking if they vomit while unconscious.

CARE Framework

Choose the time, Approach with "I" statements, Really listen, Encourage them to talk to a trusted adult.
