SECTION 1 — THE LINK BETWEEN MENTAL HEALTH & SUBSTANCE USE

Throughout this course, we have focused on what substances do to the body and brain. But there is another critical dimension that is often overlooked: the deep and complex relationship between mental health and substance use. These two issues are so frequently intertwined that health professionals have a specific term for when they occur together: a co-occurring disorder, sometimes called a dual diagnosis.

According to SAMHSA, approximately 50% of people with a substance use disorder also have a diagnosable mental health condition — and vice versa. For teenagers, this overlap is especially significant. The adolescent years are the most common period of onset for many mental health disorders, including depression, anxiety, ADHD, and trauma-related conditions. These are the same years when experimentation with substances tends to begin. This is not a coincidence.

🔄
A Bidirectional Relationship

The relationship between mental health and substance use runs in both directions. Mental health struggles can lead to substance use, and substance use can cause or worsen mental health problems. This cycle — where each condition fuels the other — is one of the main reasons co-occurring disorders are so difficult to treat and why addressing only one issue without the other is rarely effective.

SECTION 2 — SELF-MEDICATING: WHEN SUBSTANCES FEEL LIKE A SOLUTION

Self-medication refers to using substances to manage emotional pain or mental health symptoms rather than seeking professional treatment. For many people, self-medication begins as a logical — if misguided — attempt to cope. A teenager with crushing anxiety may find that alcohol quiets the nervous system enough to get through a social situation. Someone with depression may find that marijuana temporarily lifts their mood. In the short term, these substances seem to work. That perceived relief is precisely what makes them so dangerous.

While substances may temporarily reduce symptoms, they do not treat the underlying condition — and over time they make it significantly worse. Alcohol deepens depression. Marijuana increases anxiety with regular use. Stimulants cause mood crashes. Opioids blunt all emotion, including positive feelings. All addictive substances also disrupt sleep, which is critical for mental health regulation.

⚠️
Why Self-Medication Always Makes Things Worse

→ Alcohol is a depressant — using it for depression ultimately deepens depressive symptoms
→ Marijuana increases anxiety and paranoia with regular use, especially in teens
→ Stimulants cause anxiety and mood crashes when they wear off
→ Opioids blunt all emotion — including positive feelings — leaving users emptier than before
→ All addictive substances disrupt sleep, which is critical for mental health
→ The shame and secrecy of substance use adds new layers of stress and isolation

SECTION 3 — COMMON CO-OCCURRING CONDITIONS IN TEENS

Several mental health conditions are especially likely to co-occur with substance use disorders in adolescents. Understanding these connections helps us recognize warning signs — in ourselves and in others — and respond with empathy rather than judgment.

😔

Depression

Affects approximately 1 in 5 teens

Depression involves persistent sadness, hopelessness, and loss of interest in things once enjoyed. Teens with depression are significantly more likely to use alcohol and marijuana to escape emotional pain. Both are depressants that worsen symptoms over time and impair the motivation needed to seek real help.

😰

Anxiety Disorders

Most common teen mental health disorder; ~32% of adolescents

Teens with social anxiety may use alcohol as liquid courage. While alcohol temporarily reduces anxiety, regular use leads to rebound anxiety — heightened anxiety between uses — causing the disorder to worsen significantly over time.

🧠

ADHD

Teens with ADHD are 2–3× more likely to develop a SUD

The same impulsivity that is a core feature of ADHD increases the likelihood of trying and escalating substance use. Teens with undiagnosed ADHD may self-medicate with stimulants to focus or use marijuana to quiet racing thoughts. Proper ADHD treatment significantly reduces substance use risk.

💔

Trauma & PTSD

Adverse childhood experiences dramatically increase substance use risk

Trauma fundamentally alters the brain's stress-response system. Substances that dull these responses — opioids, alcohol, and benzodiazepines — become powerfully reinforcing. Trauma-informed care is a critical component of effective addiction treatment.

🌪️

Chronic Stress

An underrecognized gateway to substance use

Chronic stress from academic pressure, family conflict, or social comparison activates the same stress-response systems that drive self-medication. Teens under high stress are significantly more likely to experiment with substances, making stress management a direct form of prevention.

💡
Important: A Mental Health Condition Is a Risk Factor, Not Destiny

Millions of teens navigate depression, anxiety, ADHD, and trauma without ever developing a substance use disorder — especially when they have access to support, treatment, and healthy coping strategies. Knowledge and early intervention are the most powerful tools available.

SECTION 4 — HEALTHY COPING STRATEGIES

If substances are an unhealthy way of coping with emotional pain, the answer is not simply do not cope. The answer is learning healthy coping strategies — ones that actively improve brain chemistry, emotional regulation, and resilience over time without creating new problems.

🏃

Physical Activity

Exercise naturally releases dopamine and endorphins without harmful side effects. Even a 20-minute walk has measurable effects on anxiety and depression.

✍️

Journaling & Expression

Writing about emotions for just 10 minutes activates the prefrontal cortex and reduces emotional intensity of distressing experiences. Art and music work similarly.

🧘

Mindfulness & Breathing

Deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system — the body's rest and digest mode — directly counteracting the stress response that drives substance use.

👥

Trusted Relationships

Talking to someone you trust lowers cortisol and provides genuine emotional relief through the brain's social bonding system.

😴

Sleep

Sleep deprivation worsens depression, anxiety, and impulsive decision-making. Teens need 8–10 hours per night. Consistent sleep schedules are one of the most underrated mental health interventions.

🎯

Purpose & Goals

Having a clear sense of purpose — a goal, a passion, a community — is one of the strongest protective factors against substance use.

🚨
When to Seek Professional Help

Consider reaching out to a counselor or trusted adult if you experience any of the following for more than two weeks:

→ Persistent sadness, hopelessness, or feeling empty
→ Anxiety so severe it interferes with school, sleep, or daily life
→ Using substances to manage emotions or get through the day
→ Thoughts of self-harm or suicide
→ Significant changes in eating, sleeping, or social behavior

KEY VOCABULARY

Co-Occurring Disorder

When a substance use disorder and a mental health condition exist at the same time; also called dual diagnosis

Self-Medication

Using substances to manage mental health symptoms or emotional pain instead of seeking professional treatment

Bidirectional

Running in both directions — mental health affects substance use AND substance use affects mental health

ACEs

Adverse Childhood Experiences — traumatic events in childhood that increase risk for mental health and substance use disorders

Resilience

The ability to adapt positively in the face of adversity — a protective factor that can be actively developed

Cortisol

The body's primary stress hormone; chronically elevated cortisol is linked to both mental health disorders and increased substance use risk

Integrated Treatment

A treatment approach that addresses both a substance use disorder and a co-occurring mental health condition at the same time

Protective Factor

A condition or attribute — like strong relationships or a sense of purpose — that reduces the likelihood of negative outcomes
