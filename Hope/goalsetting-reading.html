Substance Abuse Prevention

Goal Setting, Future Planning & Staying Drug-Free

The strongest reason to stay drug-free isn't fear. It's a future you actually want to walk into. This week, you'll learn how to build that future — and how to protect it.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

✓Explain why having clear goals lowers your risk of substance use.
✓Write SMART goals across multiple areas of your life — academic, personal, athletic, social.
✓Identify how substance use can derail short-term and long-term plans.
✓Build a personal "why" — the deeper reason behind staying drug-free.
✓Create healthy habits and a support system that protect your goals long-term.

Section One

Why Goals Are the Best Drug Prevention There Is

Most drug prevention programs try to scare you. They show you damaged brains, ruined lives, courtroom photos. The problem is, fear wears off. By the time you're at a party, you've forgotten the slideshow. Fear isn't a strategy — it's a feeling.

Goals are a strategy. Researchers who study why some teens use substances and others don't have found something interesting: the biggest single predictor isn't family income, isn't IQ, isn't even peer pressure. It's whether a teen has a clear, specific picture of who they want to become.

When you have a future you can see, today's decisions get easier. Saying no isn't about willpower — it's about not wanting to throw away what you're already building.

By the Numbers
2–3×
Teens with clearly defined future goals are 2–3× less likely to use substances
10×
People who write down their goals are about 10× more likely to actually achieve them than people who just think about them

Think about athletes. Why does an Olympic swimmer turn down the drink at a party? Not because they're afraid of consequences. Because the gold medal in their head is more vivid than the moment in front of them. They have something to protect.

💡
Reframe

You don't have to be an Olympian to use this. The same logic works whether your goal is making varsity, getting into a specific college, becoming a veterinarian, or just being someone your little sister actually looks up to. A specific future protects you from generic temptations.

Section Two

How to Set a Goal That Actually Works: SMART

Most goals fail because they're too vague. "Get in shape." "Do better in school." "Be a good person." These are wishes, not goals. A real goal is one your brain can actually act on.

Use the SMART framework. Each letter forces you to make your goal more specific until it becomes something you could actually start working on tomorrow.

S

Specific

Don't say "do better in math." Say which math, how much better, and what counts as success.

Vague: "Get better at math."
Specific: "Raise my Algebra 1 grade from a C to a B by the end of the quarter."

M

Measurable

If you can't tell whether you hit it, you can't tell whether you're making progress. Numbers, dates, or clear yes/no checkpoints.

Unmeasurable: "Get fitter."
Measurable: "Run 1 mile in under 9 minutes."

A

Achievable

Stretchy enough to matter, realistic enough to actually be possible. Setting an impossible goal sets you up to give up.

Unachievable: "Make the Olympic team this year." (You're 14.)
Achievable: "Make the varsity track team next season."

R

Relevant

It should connect to who you actually are and where you actually want to go — not what someone else wants for you.

Irrelevant: Joining debate because your friend joined.
Relevant: Joining debate because you want to study law one day.

T

Time-bound

A goal without a deadline is a daydream. Pick a date. The deadline is what makes your brain start working on it.

No deadline: "Learn to play guitar."
Time-bound: "Learn 5 songs on guitar by my birthday in March."

✨ Putting It All Together

Wish:

"I want to be a better artist."

SMART goal:

"I will complete one full sketchbook (about 80 pages) of original drawings by June 1 by sketching for at least 20 minutes a day, five days a week."

Section Three

How Substance Use Quietly Steals Your Future

Substance use rarely blows up your life all at once. It does it slowly, in ways you don't notice until you look back and realize the goal you used to talk about isn't on the table anymore.

Here's what actually happens — across four areas where teens build their futures.

🎓 Academic Goals

Even occasional marijuana use during your teen years lowers your ability to retain new information for hours after the high wears off. The hippocampus — the memory-builder — is still developing until your mid-20s. Missed classes, dropped grades, and "I just can't focus anymore" don't look like addiction. They look like falling behind. Then college applications come, and the GPA you wanted is gone.

🏃 Athletic Goals

Vaping reduces your lung capacity. Alcohol disrupts the deep sleep your body needs to actually build muscle. Marijuana slows reaction time for hours. Coaches notice. Spots get given to other players. The scholarship you needed for college? It went to the kid who got 7 hours of clean sleep last night.

💼 Career & Future Goals

A drug charge on your record can disqualify you from financial aid for college, from joining the military, from many state licenses (nursing, teaching, real estate, law). You don't lose access to those careers when you're 30 and rebuild your life. You lose access at 17 because of one bad night.

❤️ Personal & Relationship Goals

The friend you were going to road-trip with after graduation. The relationship with your parents you wanted to repair. The version of yourself you saw in the mirror at 16 and liked. Substance use changes how you show up in every relationship — and the people who love you notice before you do.

⏳
The math people don't tell you

Most teens who use substances think: "I'll quit when I need to get serious." The reality is the opposite. The teen brain forms habits faster and breaks them slower than the adult brain. The longer you wait to "get serious," the harder serious gets.

Section Four

Finding Your "Why" — The Reason Behind the Reason

Goals tell you what. A "why" tells you why it matters when nobody's watching. Your why is the deeper, personal reason behind your goals — and it's what carries you through the hard nights.

A useful exercise is called 5 Whys. You start with what you want, then keep asking "why" until you hit something that genuinely matters to you.

Example: Going from a goal to a "why"
G
Goal: I want to get into nursing school.
1
Why? Because I want to be a nurse.
2
Why? Because I want to help people.
3
Why? Because when my grandma was sick, the nurses were the ones who treated her like a person.
4
Why? Because everyone deserves to feel seen at their lowest moment.
5
Why? Because I never want anyone to feel as alone as my grandma almost did.
YOUR WHY:
"Because nobody should be invisible when they need help most."

That last answer — "nobody should be invisible when they need help most" — is the kind of reason that holds up at 11 p.m. at a party when someone hands you something. The surface-level goal ("nursing school") might not be enough. The why underneath it is.

🔑
The test of a real "why"

A real why is something you'd say out loud and almost get embarrassed about because it feels too honest. If your why doesn't make you a little emotional, keep digging. You haven't hit it yet.

Section Five

The Habits and People That Protect Your Goals

Goals don't get achieved on motivation alone. Motivation is unreliable — it shows up some days and not others. What actually carries you to a goal is habits (the things you do automatically) and a support system (the people who keep you on track).

Five Habits That Protect Drug-Free Goals

1
Sleep 8+ hours. Sleep deprivation cripples judgment and makes substances look way more appealing than they are.
2
Move your body daily. Exercise gives you the same dopamine hit substances do — but builds you instead of breaking you.
3
Have one creative outlet. Music, art, writing, photography, building things. Creativity is a relief valve for stress.
4
Limit doom-scrolling. Heavy social media use is linked to higher anxiety, lower self-esteem, and higher substance use risk.
5
Plan ahead for parties. Decide what you'll say before you walk in. "I'm driving" works. So does "I'm good." Practice it out loud.

Building Your Support Squad

You don't need a huge group. You need three to four reliable people who know your goals and want you to win.

🤝 A Peer Ally

A friend who doesn't use, or who's also chosen to stay drug-free. Your "I'll text you to come pick me up" person.

👨‍👩 A Trusted Adult

Someone older — parent, aunt, coach, counselor — you'd actually call if things got hard. You picked three of these last week.

🎯 A Mentor or Role Model

Someone — in person or even just online — who is doing what you want to do. Watch them. Learn from them.

🪞 A Future-You Anchor

A photo, a vision board, a written paragraph about who you'll be in 10 years. Look at it when motivation is low.

Section Six

When You Get Off Track — And You Will

No one walks a straight line to their goals. You'll have weeks where you don't study. Months where you skip the gym. Bad nights where you make a choice you regret. Getting off track is not failing. Quitting after getting off track is failing.

When you mess up — at school, with substances, with relationships — the most useful thing you can do is run the Reset Protocol:

1. Acknowledge it without spiraling.

"I made a mistake last weekend." Not "I'm a failure." The story you tell yourself matters more than the mistake itself.

2. Figure out what happened.

Was it stress? A specific person? A specific place? You can't avoid a trigger you haven't named.

3. Tell one person.

Secrets grow. Spoken-out-loud mistakes shrink. Tell a peer ally, a trusted adult, or even just write it in a journal.

4. Take one small action today.

Don't try to fix everything. Do one thing — go to school, work out, text the friend you've been avoiding. Small actions rebuild momentum.

5. Get back to your why.

Reread it. Look at the photo. Talk to the future-you you're trying to become. The why is what restarts the engine.

🌱
The most important sentence in this lesson

You are not behind. You are exactly where someone with your story would be at this point. The version of you in 5 years is built by what you do next — not by what you've already done.

Key Vocabulary

SMART Goal

A goal that is Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound — designed to be acted on, not just imagined.

"Why"

The deeper personal reason behind a goal. The motivation that holds up when things get hard or no one is watching.

5 Whys Exercise

A technique where you ask "why" five times in a row to dig past the surface goal and discover what truly matters underneath.

Future Self

The version of you that exists 5, 10, or 20 years from now. Decisions today either get you closer to that person or further away.

Trigger

A person, place, situation, or feeling that pushes you toward a behavior you're trying to avoid. Naming triggers is the first step to managing them.

Reset Protocol

A 5-step process for getting back on track after a setback: acknowledge, identify the trigger, tell one person, take one small action, return to your why.

Support Squad

A small group of 3–4 people — peer ally, trusted adult, mentor, future-you anchor — who help you stay aligned with your goals.

Habit Stack

A set of small, repeated actions done daily or weekly that compound into long-term progress. More reliable than motivation.
